Addressing the why for Bitcoin 1.0

21st August 2021 (Written in December/Jan)

Many have covered this topic, either quite extensively with great depth or with rather scant outlining, however, the true, collective understanding as displayed through trending ideas and sentiment seems to follow rational more emblematic of the latter. This is by no means a critique of the community; the concepts involved are difficult and exciting narratives or memes will always gain the most traction. The purpose of this therefore, is to provide enough depth to see through the noise, and present Bitcoin’s need not as an investment, trading asset or digital currency, but as sound money. And the truest form of sound money existing today, outclassing gold. We’ll look first at money and then at Bitcoin, addressing why this new technology is far more important, necessary and valuable beyond general understanding.

Understanding Bitcoin as sound money first requires an understanding of sound money.

Money holds paramount importance in the functioning, growth and sustainability of any healthy society. Money, for any complex society, could even be considered a prerequisite. We see this when picturing an early human world before money, a world under the barter system**. A barter system as a means of exchange is socially restrictive, permitting the double coincidence of wants. Where any good, can only be exchanged when the other party wants that good and happens to offer something our seller wants in return. The problem of divisibility also occurs here. Consider a trade where the double coincidence of wants is met, and our two parties are looking to trade a specially crafted spear for apples (our hunter has had a bad week). The spear is far more valuable than the apples and since it cannot be subdivided without being destroyed, a satisfactory exchange might be impossible to occur; more apples beyond a certain point ≠ more value. It follows that this exchange will only take place when the immediate desire for a meal (apples) becomes greater than the value of a tool for procuring food, to a hunter nonetheless, which could be at the point of starvation.

Here we can see that in order to further specialise and divide labour, barter is not suitable. And when specialising, dividing labour and working together is the key to the growth of complex society, the participants, as our early humans did, will naturally pivot to a means of exchange to facilitate trade. The early and first intermediaries for trade being shells and stones among others is quite thoroughly explored by Nick Szabo.

With the idea that an intermediary for trade must exist as a foundational element for societal growth, we can now look at what that intermediary should ideally be. Or, what money should be and what naturally constitutes sound money. It will thus be helpful to look at markets and see what can naturally occur. We shall call our generalised market the ‘Earl Market’, as it consists of early humans who only trade with commodities (basic goods) at this point. In looking at this market we will recognise how gold, became the world’s chosen intermediary and chosen form of money.

In the Earl Market, participants can trade anything they produce or acquire. We soon find that certain commodities will have an increased demand due to their relative scarcity. More people will be willing to trade their goods for a rare, distinctive precious metal like gold than for seashells. Because of this, the owners of such goods will find it easier in the market to obtain what they truly want whether that be food, materials, garments etc. Those who do not have such goods, highly tradeable goods, and have illiquid goods instead, will look to trade not for their wants/needs, but to come under possession of commodities more tradeable. To thereby help them get to their wants/needs more easily. It follows that commodities in this market, are not only worth their value to any given individual, but the tradability of a good will add to its value, as any owner will always be closer to acquiring what they truly want/need. Extending this market through time and to other markets far and wide, our participants will also consider the portability and durability of any goods they may wish to acquire, ensuring future needs/want can be met regardless of geography. Increasing the value of durability and transportability for tradable goods.

At first, only a limited number of participants will discover the value in a commodity that will always bring them closer to acquiring whichever good they want/need. But any such good, as more participants realise its value beyond intrinsic qualities and instead in what others and they themselves can gain in function from these qualities, will become widely accepted. Widely accepted for its function in tradability as well as its function as a store of value for future trade. As more people use the commodity, more others will realise why they too should be using the commodity. Human history has seen many tested commodities: shells, stones and even rice, yet gold and other precious metals were naturally found to have the best suited qualities for money all over the world. This gradual genesis of a medium of exchange, as best written in Carl Menger’s ‘On the Origins of Money’ is how we can understand gold becoming the de facto commodity of the Earl Market and that of the human world. And to close with a direct quote.

“there is no better method of enlightening any one about his economic

interests than that he perceive the economic success of those who

use the right means to secure their own.”

No central authority chose gold; the market did. Showing, the counterintuitive notion, that money, will naturally arise in the free market as a commodity. And while it isn’t used as a direct means of exchange today, as sound money, gold still holds value. The qualities needed for sound money as displayed through the emergence of gold are: fungibility (interchangeable because they are identical), divisibility, recognisability, scarcity, portability and durability. And through these qualities we see how sound money can and by definition, functions as a medium of exchange, store of value and furthermore a unit of account. These qualities and functions define sound money, and hold the standard for a best store of our economic energy.

The term sound money is necessary, because what we recognise as money today derives value in a different way. The money we recognise today is actually Fiat and defined as:

“Fiat money is a currency established as money, often by government regulation, that does not have intrinsic value. Fiat money does not have use value, and has value only because a government maintains its value, or because parties engaging in exchange agree on its value.”

Presentation of a problem that has Bitcoin as a solution

The meaning of money has been perverted through history. Hence the need to differentiate between fiat and sound. However, a general definition can be understood as follows. Money is the title we give to any instrument commonly used as: a store of value, means of exchange and unit of account. Defining money through its functions. The key aspect of this definition however, is that any instrument can hold the title of money, if used for these functions. However, said instrument may not necessarily possess the qualities needed for these functions, as seen in gold, and can therefore lose the title of money.

Made especially evident in room 68 of The British Museum, a graveyard of sorts for all the historic instruments that have lost their title of money after losing value (As highlighted in The Hidden secrets of money by Mike Maloney). Gold, having been found through the market for having specific qualities, still holds value and function as money centuries later. The significant difference between gold and any room 68 inhabitant, is that use was never induced by a central authority. Every single currency, given its title of money through a central authority (fiat money), will become worthless through manipulation by that central authority. Consequently, we must come to accept, the fiat currencies, holding the title of money today, are all feeling the gradual yet certain pull of room 68. Somewhere along the way, centrally issued currencies lose integral qualities as compared to gold and henceforth, fail to fulfil the functions needed of money.

Now then, why and where does this happen? And could this time be different? To understand why this happens we will continue our brief history of money production and spot why centrally issued currencies are consistently doomed to retirement within room 68.

Once gold, amongst other precious metals were in use, centralised, trusted actors were then able to step in and help stabilise the process of trade and offset limits to gold through processes such as coinage. Imprinting the metal with certification of weight and denoting a worth for exchange. This helps alleviate the need for people to weigh their own metals in providing function as a unit of account and increases the convenience of sound money. This process, therefore, adds to the value of certified coins in comparison to the raw metals, giving the minter profits for their efforts. When the process of certification can create value, we can see that the certificates themselves can be used without a necessary integration with the metal it represents. Alongside the fact that larger scale transport and storage of gold can prove inefficient as economies grow, a reason for central storage and ‘substitute money’ can be seen. With central authorities involved for minting (mainly governments), and banking (storing gold deposits and issuing money substitutes), we can see where our first distortions will occur.

This is by no means a complete account; we needn’t go over the debasement of coins and episodes of hyperinflation around Ancient Rome. Here, the video series Hidden secrets of money gives a highly engaging outline amongst other teachings. The most important takeaway here is that governments will never fail to debase currency and artificially increase the supply. Using this extra currency to pay for extravagancies (particularly war) it couldn’t otherwise afford through taxation alone. However, since debasement of coins can be slow and limited, governments found better ways to inflate the money supply by cooperating with fractional reserve banks. A couple of definitions can be helpful here.

  • >Inflation in this context means “An extension of the nominal quantity of any medium of exchange beyond the quantity that would have been produced on the free market” as defined in The Ethics of money production by Jörg Guido Hülsmann. Which goes on to give the more present-day definition too “A lasting increase of the price level…/ reduction of the purchasing power of money”. Through this definition we can see the negative impact of inflation on savings and on those who hold the inflated currency.
  • >Fractional reserve banking is the practice of banks holding only a portion of customer deposits in relation to notes issued representing those deposits; to allow for increased lending and an ‘expansion of the economy’. The increased lending however is what grants the bank additional profits beyond what could be achieved through reserves alone.

The process of moving to fiat, from notes that represent quantities of gold (hence the names pound, mark, franc) can now be summarised as follows. The government will first establish a monopoly on precious metals, quashing their use as a means of exchange. Then grant legal tender status to the ‘money substitutes’ or notes of a privileged fractional reserve bank (the bank of the government, to later become a ‘central bank’). Legal tender status means that the notes must be accepted regardless of any former contracts (contracts where gold is owed). Establishing a national currency through law and ending the issuance of private currencies. These now overvalued notes, will further drive the undervalued money out. People will trade with and circulate the ‘bad money’ and hoard any ‘good money’, known as Gresham’s Law. At this point, people are using their national, central banks legal tender notes that represent gold only fractionally reserved. Eventually this privileged bank will be allowed to indefinitely decline redemption of the sound money that these notes were formerly representing (‘a removal from the gold standard’) in a suspension of payments. Hereby making paper money or fiat. As this monetary system now stands, this privileged bank can effectively produce/print money at no cost at the will of government by providing them loans backed by nothing; it’s just paper that only has value while citizens accept it. This process works to create a system where governments, through central banks, can create currency for themselves to the detriment of people using the currency. This last happened in 1971 when the US dollar, the worlds reserve currency was made to be no longer redeemable in gold. This process of paper money production is more thoroughly articulated by Hüllsmann and some drastic effects are fascinatingly visualised through graphs featured here at wtfhappenedin1971.com.

Now to close this segment with some key followings. Once money becomes fiat, it can be created at will, hence the explosion of government debt. This excessive creation of fiat, removes the scarce quality needed for sound money. If it can be made at no cost, and profits those in control, it will soon be worth less than the paper it’s printed on. This system only lasts as long as people are willing to continue with the lie that their currency has any lasting value. As currency devalues, people will begin to need more for the goods and services they provide, our modern definition of inflation. Think of rising prices for household goods over the years. This increase will happen exponentially until prices are going up so fast that currency not spent today, is currency lost tomorrow. When this happens, currencies are hyperinflating as they have done most recently in Zimbabwe and Venezuela. Destroying wealth and ruining livelihoods. Fiat currency requires full trust in the same institutions who profit from its creation at no immediate cost. This is because the true cost is offset to the population who unknowingly keep faith and savings in a continually debased form of money. With that in mind, you can count on your central bank and government to curtail this issue and ensure wealth isn’t destroyed, unlike failed empires and governments through history, which they might, or you can look to another solution.

Understanding Bitcoin as a form of sound money

Bitcoin is complicated, however it is no more complicated than money itself. And if you understand enough about money to see any issue today, you can easily understand enough about bitcoin to see it as a potential solution.

Bitcoin has two meanings, most easily understood as a peer-to-peer electronic cash system, and a unit of exchange within the system. We’ll set up some context by way of the whitepaper. Published in 2008, the whitepaper was written by an anonymous individual or individuals by the name Satoshi Nakamoto. The project itself was then released as open-source software in early 2009. Hereby introducing the first key concept of Bitcoin, decentralisation. Being open source means that the code is openly available to anyone. This may seem like a security flaw, however this actually works to increase security and trust; we can always see exactly how it works and ensure its operability. Consider companies like Facebook, who through their business model and private codebase have come under scrutiny for shady advertising technology. If you can see exactly how it works, you can trust it. More so, you don’t need to trust anything because the technology is visible in the code. Open-source development works to pool resources from anyone with an interest in the project at hand. The best of open-source development leads to the growth of projects through sheer passion and world benefit without financial incentive. This worked for Linux, which quietly took over the world, and It may work for Bitcoin too as suggested in a Ted-talk by Rusty Russel on a history of Open-source.

Having no identifiable creator/s means no centralised ownership and the project truly belongs to the people. Satoshi Nakamoto guided development for the first few years, while needed, and then disappeared in 2011, giving way to a fully decentralised system for the world to build upon.

We can already see how the decentralised nature, from conception differs to the inhabitants of room 68, now let’s look at the technology that makes Bitcoin. Moving on from the whitepaper, considered a technical paper, we’ll look to understand what makes Bitcoin pioneering. The first point to be made is that bitcoin solves a long-standing consensus problem which can be understood through the Byzantine generals’ problem. Many overlook the fact that Bitcoin is cutting-edge in this regard alone. The Byzantine generals’ problem involves a number of generals, separated geographically, trying to reach consensus on when to attack an enemy. If consensus cannot be met the attack fails. Despite omitting details here, in understanding the sense of the problem, James A. Donald put it best.

“It is not sufficient that everyone knows X. We also need everyone to know that everyone knows X, and that everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows X — which, as in the Byzantine Generals’ problem, is the classic hard problem of distributed data processing.”

Without a solution to this problem, digital payment systems cannot exist without a trusted, centralised entity ensuring that no one spends the same money in multiple places (double spend) and thereby creates money through dishonest transactions. All parties need a way of knowing where the supply of money is, so that they can tell when money can be rightfully exchanged. Bitcoins solution to this problem, or its solution to double spend, allows it to provide peer-to-peer digital payments with any trusted party involved.

Now, to understand exactly how bitcoin does this, and the interesting concepts within the core technology, much more writing would need to be dedicated as many new concepts must be introduced. Keeping things simpler, addressing some misunderstandings of Bitcoin can provide enough outline.

The first instinctual question, that may arise for some is: what is tangible about bitcoin, how and where does it actually exist?

The misunderstanding of bitcoin in this regard is often propagated by media depiction. You often see gold coins engraved with symbolic B’s, which are actually meaningless and misguided visualisations. A better means of understanding is through the word itself ‘Bit-coin’ with a bit being a basic unit of information. Rather than thinking about its presence through a coin, think about it as information. This information is stored publicly and held by everyone within the Bitcoin ecosystem. When you personally own Bitcoin, you’ll have a cryptographically generated password (private key) that gives you alone the ability to transact with another party and update the whole ecosystem to show that Bitcoin has been moved from one address (public key) to another. Or more rather that the general information has changed. This is done through a decentralised ledger technology called a blockchain and can therefore be more appropriately visualised through transactions-grouped-in-blocks-connected-by-a-strong-chain-in-a-public-ledger-of-information. The reliability of any new transaction comes from the fact that every transaction since the beginning is publicly stored allowing us to ensure that said Bitcoin is actually under the possession of the party transacting with it. It follows that if all your technology, personally used to deal with your Bitcoin were unfortunately destroyed, stolen or lost, the public ledger would still identify your Bitcoin as within your address. Consequently, with your private key, you could continue to transact from any new device.

Another question that often arises is: what gives Bitcoin its value, since it doesn’t do anything how can it have value?

Here we can look at the creation of new bitcoin, involving a process we call mining. Miners group transactions together (information updates) and compete to form the newest block to be cryptographically linked in the blockchain (the public ledger). The miners alongside nodes (Nodes will hold the entire blockchains information and validate transactions) are therefore sustaining the entire system, but not for free of course. It’s the first miner to show ‘proof of work’ and solve a cryptographic puzzle, that will publish the latest block and be rewarded a set amount of Bitcoin for their efforts. Proof of work requires a lot of energy, however being rewarded in bitcoin makes it profitable. The energy cost required from proof of work also makes it difficult to change a transaction once on the blockchain (beyond a few blocks). It’s worth noting that miners also get paid in transaction fees for the transactions they store.

Now it still might be hard to grasp why Bitcoin has value, which is okay because there are some key ‘monetary policy’ elements we haven’t touched yet. Proof of work is difficulty adjusted, meaning no matter how strong mining computers get, or the number of miners competing, it will always take an average of 10 minutes for the next block to be mined generating new supply. This new supply, as rewarded to the miners halves every four years. Most importantly however, there is a limited supply of Bitcoin capped at 21 million to ever be mined. This makes Bitcoin scarce. Therefore, with around 18 million in circulation now, we can also see that the last Bitcoin will be mined some time around 2140, where miners will then get paid through transaction fees alone.

While there may be no intrinsic value to Bitcoin like gold (nice to look at), it holds value by way of the cost needed for Bitcoin to exist and its limited supply. Not counting of course, the value given to it by the market.

The qualities needed for sound money are all present within Bitcoin and actually surpass gold. It’s more easily divisible with the most important denomination being Satoshis/Sats at 0.00000001 bitcoin. Plus, it’s more easily portable with ownership being in the form of private keys (passwords). The constraints on those two qualities were the reasons for continual centralisation of gold in minting and in storage. In this regard Bitcoin reigns supreme as a more accessible and decentralised instrument of exchange and store of value. The one reigning quality of gold however, is an unyielding durability through time. Bitcoin has yet to prove itself in this regard however since it can also live without the internet (satellite, radio, SMS…) and is decentralised, destroying the ecosystem becomes unfeasible.

The technology of Bitcoin is not only cutting-edge in the problems it solves, but in being born of the internet, it’s most aptly suited to the world we live in and future needs of money. With only 11 years Bitcoin is currently establishing itself as a store of value. Reputation as sound money is mostly held back through effort needed to understand it. However, as the price keeps rising and more people come to recognize the value in Bitcoin, more and more people will want ownership, as in the previous quote.

“there is no better method of enlightening any one about his economic

interests than that he perceive the economic success of those who

use the right means to secure their own.”

Lest we not also forget, supply is capped at 21 million.

Bitcoin as it stands today

It wouldn’t make sense to address the why of Bitcoin without looking at the why nots and common arguments against. One of this first, is on the wastefulness in the energy consumption used to mine Bitcoin. However, the externality incurred, in negative environmental effects, is exactly the same as that which powers modern society. The biggest difference being, this energy is used to create a decentralised financial system for the world to store value against failing centralised systems. Imagine if all the savings and real value lost in hyperinflation, such as in Zimbabwe, were transferred into Bitcoin. The net gain to humanity is far greater than plastic bags; this argument holds negligible weight.

Another point, more valid however, is that Bitcoin is currently inefficient as a means of exchange, as when transactions get congested, fees balloon. This happens because there’s a limit to the number of transactions that can be mined in a single block and miners will prioritise transactions yielding higher fees. Bitcoin developers, as it’s an open-source development project, are currently working on solutions to this problem through the ‘lightning network’. Which proposes to add another layer for scalability. Any proprietor of this point, as an argument against Bitcoin, will almost always have some stake in the current financial system that is provably unsustainable. Choosing ignorance is not an argument; the growth of the Bitcoin community and wider community too, who believe and who work to make Satoshi Nakamoto’s vision reality, has been and continues to be the most bullish signal for future dominance.  

The volatility of Bitcoins price, with price action often being an introduction to Bitcoin, will deter some through the ideas that: Bitcoin cannot be a safe investment, the price is too speculative or that it’s a risk-on asset. If one thing is to be understood through this writing, it’s that Bitcoin should not be considered an investment but as a form of money itself. And a form of money still in its price discovery stage, where the market, that being the global population with access to the internet, is still trying to determine how valuable it is and what they are willing to trade for it. If the current global population of the internet decided they wanted to own some Bitcoin, that would be 5 billion people looking to trade for a max supply of 21 million Bitcoins (18 million already in circulation). It follows, that once Bitcoin has found a relative price, selling back into fiat could even be redundant. During price discovery, transacting with bitcoin can be risky as you may end up paying for the most expensive pizza in the world. As did Laszlo Hanyecz when he purchased two pizzas for 10,000 Bitcoin in 2010, which at the time of writing is worth $190,000,000.

In considering where the price may go, people like to consider that if bitcoin had the current market cap of gold, the price for one would be around $380,000 - $480,000. This is of course just a rough calculation not counting how much the world could change between now and that point.

One change that can be certain to occur rather soon, is increased regulation as governments worldwide react in some form or other, to that which cannot be controlled, provides sovereignty and destabilises control. Central bank digital currencies are being looked at worldwide, with adoption in The Bahamas and China being the farthest in rollout amongst major nations. The technologies used compared to Bitcoin however, are quite different and often vary. CBDC’S are really just attempts at extending and advancing monetary and fiscal policy as currencies collapse. Being attempts, they can easily fail and will need extreme changes to the world we live in (cash removal), in order to be adopted as intended. Further regulation cryptocurrencies themselves however, is surely coming, but it’s worth remembering that Bitcoin is decentralised and cannot be stopped itself. Most regulation will be around companies operating within the ecosystem.

The true beauty in Bitcoin however, and why it reigns supreme above gold is that it takes into account the amount of change that occurs in the world we live in. Any roadblock in the adoption of Bitcoin becomes a problem to be solved through the open-source nature of its design. The cryptocurrency landscape ushered in by Bitcoin is rapidly growing with projects providing innovation in wide ranging fields. Consider Ethereum, currently seeing a lot of development in the burgeoning world of decentralised finance (DeFi), but at the same time, working to extend the ethos as brought to us by Satoshi Nakamoto.

This is kind of technology that provides its most important function by changing the way the world thinks. Thanks to the vision of Bitcoin, through the internet, there is a global market for sound money. As a pioneering technology, and proof of concept, a replacement could theoretically be created tomorrow. However, when looking for a store of economic energy, there is a clear winner for the world. It’s the best form of sound money we’ve ever seen and it already belongs to you.

As presented in ‘The Myth of Barter’, Chapter 2 of David Graebers Debt: The First 5,000 years, more likely we had the existence of promises and debts. Highlighting that transacting parties would generally have relationships. The Barter system however is still widely used for understanding money.

 

Dipping my toes into Web Development with the creation of this Site 1.0

3rd January 2021

As soon as I realised I might end up creating a website, I started taking note of what I was doing. Initially, inspiration wasn’t web development per se, however my desire to learn JavaScript led to me dipping my toes into the WebDev world and here we are. Hooray... Since everything’s recorded and I have further plans for this site and my skills as a developer, writing up how I got here could prove valuable in the future.

I started with the Odin Project and it was from here I started learning the basics of HTML & CSS (literally all I know is basics right now) with the best secondary resources as discovered through the Odin project, being Shayhowe.com and Mozilla Web Docs.

Once I reached the task of recreating the Google homepage as part of the Odin Project, I decided to opt for something where I could display more of my own creative flair with the new skills I had learnt. And since I have plans to write articles anyway, having recently written ‘Addressing the Why for Bitcoin’ I thought a blog would do nicely.

Thinking forward, as I intended this to be a Website I would in fact use, I didn’t like the idea of having to edit the code every time I wanted to publish a new article. I don’t hardcode tweets onto twitter; I log in, there’s a nice little section for tweeting and then I go spew foolishness. Here I realised a CMS or content management system might be better for my needs. Something like WordPress. However, since that would defeat the purpose of my Web-Development project I decided to go along with the process only marginally jaded. Focusing on styling and a framework for which more could be added.

Starting with style I first wanted to create a dark website, black background white text. However, the large majority of the Blogs I looked at for inspiration, the ones I read, were all white and I wondered if there was any true benefit. And unsurprisingly Dark UI isn’t the best choice when it comes to Blog-type content.

Having lost more than half of my brand identity I started thinking about the design of my blog and as I had learnt online decided to sketch it out on paper first. The name Un_Defined came to me by way of divine intervention, as a perfect fit to replace my placeholder/typo of UNdecided in my HTML.

When it came to fleshing out the page two YouTube videos were particularly helpful. My Nav-Bar Master in addition to my responsive CSS Grid God were my two angels, for without them entering my life, I may well have regressed and created a mock-up Google Homepage instead.

Once the site was fully marked up and the styling had been added I looked for ways to publish it. Through Mozilla Web Docs I learnt that you could actually do this through GitHub, which would be a perfect fit since I could populate my empty GitHub in the absence of a Google Homepage replica.

When you publish through GitHub pages your website must be static rather than dynamic. In looking at the differences between Static and Dynamic websites I began to see the power in Html and CSS and why writing it off for the use of CMS can only stem from ignorance. Further plans for this site actually include developing it into the latter to add more functionality and depth.

At this stage I wanted my own domain name rather than the bulky domain name as offered by GitHub. Seeing that you could add your own domain name to your GitHub hosted website was great and I eventually did this, not through google domains, but through the first few YouTube results’ favourite in Dynadot. ‘undefined.london’ was a nice find and was a good enough price in my opinion. ‘.com’ and the other favourites were taken but this works perfectly for now.

I was practically finished here, however I started to question the negatives to hosting with GitHub; It seemed too good of a deal. In learning about some of the downsides of control, speed, security, I realised they weren’t particularly impactful to me right now. The next iteration of this site would solve these perhaps as I move off of GitHub and to a paid host.

Speaking of, the 1.0 in the title is in no way meaningless I have full intention of adding to and quite dramatically changing the way this site operates and furthermore, writing about it. I will use this site and my desire to upgrade it, not only as incentive in becoming a full stack developer but also as a vessel for the many ideas that require its growth from a static Web blog hosted by GitHub to whatever mutation I’ve fathered in at 9.0.

Next on The Odin Project for me: Rock, Paper, Scissors in JavaScript. Hooray...

Edit: So unfortunately the site as I has had setup from this point was not running. I was getting this message when I believed I should be able to access it. Looking back at how to point a google domain to GitHub pages I realised I was missing the step involving any IP address (GitHub’s). And as much as it brings pangs about mine heart the easiest way to rectify this issue was to cave in to Big Tech and use Google Domains just like the tutorial. However the DynaDot UI wasn’t half as clear or easy to use. Google Domains should’ve been a no-brainer maybe?

Also, when troubleshooting, the GitHub Docs page already had everything outlined. But I had overlooked it. The lesson learnt here being the documentation provided should probably be checked regardless of whether you believe you know what you’re doing or not. In this case I could’ve saved myself from unnecessarily buying undefined.london which I still own; I have no doubt overspent now. However since undefined.london is mine for the year and I prefer it to nicodeth.me, I might switch at some point with some more understanding.
